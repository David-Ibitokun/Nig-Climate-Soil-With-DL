# Chapter 5: Conclusion and Recommendations

## 5.1 Introduction

This chapter presents the concluding part of the study on climate change impacts on food security in Nigeria. It summarizes the major findings generated from the TCN-MLP model, explains the implications of those findings for agricultural planning, and offers recommendations for policy, practice, and future research.

The study focused on four major crops, namely cassava, maize, rice, and yam, across six geopolitical regions in Nigeria. Using historical climate and yield data, the model estimated crop response under a set of climate stress scenarios and translated the outputs into regional vulnerability and food security risk measures.

## 5.2 Summary of the Study

The study was designed to answer three central questions. First, it sought to determine how projected climate stress conditions affect crop yield in Nigeria. Second, it examined whether climate vulnerability differs across regions and crops. Third, it investigated how model outputs can be translated into a practical food security risk interpretation.

To address these questions, a hybrid TCN-MLP architecture was trained on climate-yield data covering the period 1999 to 2023. The model combined temporal climate sequence learning with categorical embeddings and trend-based features. Scenario analysis was then used to examine the effect of warming, drought, flooding, and compound stress on crop yield.

## 5.3 Major Findings

The main findings of the study are summarized as follows:

1. Climate stress has a substantial negative effect on crop yield in Nigeria, especially when warming and drought occur together.
2. Compound heat-drought stress produced the strongest decline in predicted yield across the tested scenarios.
3. Climate risk is unevenly distributed across the country, with South-West, South-East, and North-Central showing higher vulnerability in the model results.
4. Cassava emerged as the most climate-sensitive crop, while yam appeared comparatively more stable.
5. Maize and rice were also affected by climate stress, but their sensitivity was lower than cassava in the project output.
6. The composite food security risk score showed that regions with higher yield losses and greater instability require priority adaptation attention.

These findings indicate that climate change will likely intensify existing differences in agricultural productivity unless targeted adaptation actions are implemented.

## 5.4 Conclusions

Based on the analysis carried out in this study, the following conclusions can be drawn.

First, the trained TCN-MLP model demonstrated that climate variables are strongly associated with crop yield changes in Nigeria. The scenario outputs showed that rising temperature and rainfall stress can reduce yield significantly, while compound stress creates the most severe threat.

Second, the results confirm that food security vulnerability is not uniform across the country. Some regions are more exposed to climate stress because of their higher baseline volatility and stronger negative yield response under adverse scenarios. This means that climate adaptation policy should be spatially differentiated rather than applied uniformly.

Third, crop response differs across the four staples studied. Cassava was the most sensitive crop in the model, maize and rice showed moderate sensitivity, and yam was relatively resilient. This ranking suggests that crop planning should consider both climate exposure and crop-specific response characteristics.

Fourth, the food security risk score provides a useful way to convert model outputs into a planning-oriented metric. By combining extreme stress response, drought response, and stability deterioration, the study offers a clearer basis for prioritizing intervention.

Overall, the study shows that climate change is a direct and measurable threat to food security in Nigeria and that data-driven modelling can support more informed adaptation planning.

## 5.5 Recommendations

### 5.5.1 Policy Recommendations

1. National and state agricultural agencies should adopt region-specific climate adaptation strategies rather than a single nationwide intervention plan.
2. Food security planning should prioritize South-West, South-East, and North-Central because the model identified these regions as more vulnerable.
3. Climate-risk analytics should be integrated into agricultural policy design, extension planning, and food reserve management.
4. Irrigation development, weather advisory services, and climate-smart crop support should be expanded in high-risk zones.

### 5.5.2 Crop-Level Recommendations

1. Cassava breeding and dissemination programs should prioritize drought tolerance and yield stability under heat stress.
2. Maize production should focus on heat-tolerant hybrids, improved planting calendars, and moisture conservation practices.
3. Rice production should emphasize water management, efficient irrigation, and variety-site matching.
4. Yam should be maintained as a resilience-supporting crop while further studies examine the traits that contribute to its relative stability.

### 5.5.3 Farm-Level Recommendations

1. Farmers should adopt moisture conservation practices such as mulching, early planting, and soil water retention methods.
2. Crop diversification should be encouraged to reduce dependence on a single highly sensitive crop.
3. Access to seasonal weather forecasts should be improved so that farmers can adjust planting decisions in advance.
4. Smallholder farmers should be supported with practical climate adaptation training and access to improved seed varieties.

### 5.5.4 Institutional Recommendations

1. Agricultural extension services should be strengthened to improve the transfer of climate information to farming communities.
2. Research institutions should continue to develop and test climate-resilient crop varieties suited to Nigerian conditions.
3. Insurance and safety-net programs should be expanded to protect farmers in highly vulnerable regions.
4. Government and development partners should support data systems that can monitor climate risk, yield variation, and adaptation progress over time.

## 5.6 Limitations of the Study

The study has some limitations that should be considered when interpreting the results.

1. The scenario analysis is based on feature-space perturbation and is not a full climate-physics simulation.
2. The model is trained on historical data from 1999 to 2023, so its reliability is bounded by the patterns present in that period.
3. The results do not fully capture soil variability, pest pressure, market shocks, and farm management differences at a fine spatial scale.
4. The flooding scenario produced an unusually large positive response in the model, indicating that extreme extrapolation should be interpreted cautiously.
5. The food security risk score is a project-specific index and should be treated as a decision-support measure rather than a universal standard.

## 5.7 Suggestions for Future Research

Future studies can improve on this work in several ways.

1. Independent field validation should be used to compare model predictions with observed yield outcomes under climate stress.
2. Climate projections from global or regional climate models should be incorporated to support longer-term forecasting.
3. Additional variables such as soil fertility, pest incidence, input use, and irrigation coverage should be included in future datasets.
4. The model can be extended to additional crops and finer administrative scales for more detailed planning.
5. Future research should evaluate the socioeconomic dimension of food security, including access, affordability, and household coping capacity.
6. A deployment-ready dashboard could be developed to support continual policy monitoring and climate-risk communication.

## 5.8 Final Remark

The study demonstrates that climate change poses a real and measurable challenge to food security in Nigeria. By combining deep learning with scenario analysis and risk interpretation, the project provides a practical framework for identifying vulnerable crops and regions. The findings support targeted adaptation planning and emphasize the need for timely intervention to protect agricultural productivity and food system resilience.
