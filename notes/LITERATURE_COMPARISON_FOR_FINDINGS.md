# Literature Comparison for Project Findings (Chapter 4 - Item 3)

## Purpose of This Note
This document supports Item 3 from the chapter improvement plan: compare this project's findings with recent evidence from Nigeria, West Africa, and Africa-wide literature, then explain how to discuss the results in a B.Tech thesis defense.

## 1) Your Core Findings to Compare
From the project outputs (TCN-MLP scenario analysis):
1. Compound heat-drought stress is the strongest threat (about -53.5% mean yield change under the extreme scenario).
2. Vulnerability is regionally uneven (South-West, South-East, and North-Central higher risk; North-West and North-East lower risk).
3. Crop sensitivity differs strongly (cassava highest sensitivity in this model; yam relatively resilient).
4. Food security risk is amplified by both yield loss and instability (risk score combines extreme stress, drought response, and variability).

## 2) External Evidence and What It Says

### Source A: IPCC AR6 WGII, Chapter 9 (Africa), 2022
Reference link: https://www.ipcc.ch/report/ar6/wg2/chapter/chapter-9/

Key points relevant to your project:
1. Climate risks to African food production increase with warming and become high around 1.5C and above.
2. IPCC reports climate-linked declines in crop productivity in parts of sub-Saharan Africa, including maize losses.
3. For West Africa, the chapter highlights meaningful risk to staple systems under combined heat and drought conditions.
4. The report stresses compounding hazards and differential vulnerability across regions and livelihoods.

How this compares to your findings:
- Strong agreement: your extreme scenario (heat + drought) causing the largest yield loss is fully consistent with IPCC's compounding-risk framing.
- Strong agreement: your uneven regional risk ranking aligns with IPCC's vulnerability concept (risk depends on exposure + sensitivity + adaptive capacity, not climate hazard alone).

### Source B: SOFI 2025 (FAO/IFAD/UNICEF/WFP/WHO), 2025
Reference link: https://doi.org/10.4060/cd6008en
Supporting news summary: https://www.fao.org/newsroom/detail/global-hunger-declines--but-rises-in-africa-and-western-asia--un-report/en

Key points relevant to your project:
1. Hunger trends improved globally, but worsened in many parts of Africa and Western Asia.
2. Africa remains disproportionately affected by food insecurity burden.
3. Food price inflation and shocks reduce access to nutritious diets, especially in low-income settings.

How this compares to your findings:
- Strong agreement: your projected yield declines under stress provide a production-side explanation for why food security pressure can persist in Africa even when global averages improve.
- Added insight: your model explains local production vulnerability, while SOFI explains access/affordability stress; together they form a complete food security narrative (availability + access).

### Source C: World Bank Climate Change Knowledge Portal - Nigeria
Reference link: https://climateknowledgeportal.worldbank.org/country/nigeria

Key points relevant to your project:
1. Nigeria shows strong climate variability across regions and seasons.
2. Future climate projections indicate warming trends, with risk implications for agriculture and exposed populations.
3. The portal emphasizes risk as an interaction of hazard, exposure, and vulnerability.

How this compares to your findings:
- Agreement: your regional risk differences match the risk-framework idea that vulnerability and exposure shape outcomes, not just temperature/rainfall changes.
- Agreement: your adaptation recommendations (irrigation, climate information, resilient varieties) align with resilience planning logic used in climate-risk platforms.

## 3) Synthesis: What This Means for Your Thesis Claim
Your project is well aligned with mainstream evidence and contributes by adding location-specific, crop-specific, and model-quantified projections for Nigeria.

### What is confirmed by literature
1. Climate stress threatens food production and risk increases with warming.
2. Compound shocks (heat + drought) are particularly damaging.
3. Food security outcomes are uneven across geographies and social systems.

### What your work adds
1. A reproducible ML workflow for scenario testing on Nigerian crop data.
2. Quantified risk ranking by region and crop.
3. A practical risk index that can be used for prioritizing adaptation interventions.

## 4) How to Talk About These Findings in Defense (Ready Script)
Use this sequence when presenting:

1. Start with the problem:
"Recent international evidence shows Africa remains highly exposed to climate-food insecurity risks, even where global averages appear to improve."

2. Position your method:
"This project operationalizes that concern in a Nigeria-specific way by using a TCN-MLP model and controlled climate perturbation scenarios."

3. State the key result:
"The model shows compound heat-drought stress gives the largest expected losses, with strong regional inequality in risk."

4. Validate with literature:
"This pattern is consistent with IPCC AR6 Africa findings on compounding climate risks and with SOFI's evidence that food insecurity pressure remains high in Africa."

5. Explain policy relevance:
"The result supports targeted adaptation by region and crop rather than one-size-fits-all intervention."

6. Close with scientific caution:
"These are model-based scenario projections, not deterministic forecasts, so they should inform planning together with field validation and socioeconomic data."

## 5) Suggested Paragraph to Insert into Chapter 4 (Item 3)
The findings from this study are consistent with broader evidence from recent regional and global assessments. IPCC AR6 (Africa chapter) indicates that climate risks to food production in Africa rise sharply with warming and are intensified by compounding hazards, particularly heat and drought. This aligns with the current model output, where the compound extreme scenario produces the strongest yield decline. In addition, SOFI 2025 reports that food insecurity pressures remain severe in Africa despite modest global improvements, supporting the practical relevance of projected production shocks for vulnerable populations. Finally, the World Bank Climate Change Knowledge Portal for Nigeria highlights that climate risk emerges from the interaction of hazard, exposure, and vulnerability, which is reflected in this study's uneven regional risk profile. Taken together, these comparisons support the validity and policy relevance of the project's conclusions while emphasizing the need for continuous local validation.
